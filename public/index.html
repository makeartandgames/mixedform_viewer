resting wasted forward pleading down
the red and peeling counter wood. She is folding
now unfolding, staring past my eyes.

If it’s a silent day one may
simply walk into air;
Elanor would say. For Elanor Vance 
was thirty-two when she drove 
the car in which her father
died, and ever since, she had lived
by revelation, in the lean
bitter-edged world which begets
such truth. Can you see it? She would say 
to the counter girl,
who slim as a whippet passing
at a swift walk on a tangential way
by the beggars, gave nothing up,
but planned a speech:
Mother, you see, flesh will not keep, 
time’s pocket is picked; the lock-­
shaken lute strings jig no music mute
from their wooden womb and I
am twenty-three now, and slaved
to wants I call my own and hold a deed
to hell with my printed name 
and need you very little, anymore.

bury the dead. Envy the last screaming for what it sells,
